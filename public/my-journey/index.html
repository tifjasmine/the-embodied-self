Before the work

For a long time, I lived in the in-between.

For as long as I can remember, I felt like I was trying to figure out where I belonged. Too much for some spaces, not enough for others. Not fully this, not fully that. Just floating, searching, and trying to become whatever version of me might finally be accepted.

I spent so much of my childhood trying to read the room. What did people want from me? Who did I need to be to feel wanted, safe, chosen, or loved? By adolescence, that ache turned into rebellion, acting out, and looking for belonging in places that could never really give it to me.

Underneath all of it was a quieter truth: I did not believe I was worth loving. Not pretty enough. Not good enough. Not wanted enough. Not worthy of softness, care, gentleness, or peace.

The ache underneath

I know what it feels like to be far from yourself.

There were moments in my life that felt incredibly dark. Moments when I did not know how I would come out of what I was feeling. But even then, there was something tender inside of me that refused to give up.

That part of me did not have all the words yet. She did not have the tools, the language, the support, or the self-compassion. But she was still there. Waiting. Reaching. Wanting a way back.

My healing did not begin with a perfect breakthrough. It began slowly, through the body, through movement, through breath, and through the first fragile moments of realizing I could feel something other than shame.

My healing did not start by understanding everything. It started by learning how to listen.

Movement gave me access to parts of myself I had spent years avoiding. It helped me feel present, strong, soft, emotional, grounded, and alive in a way I had never known before.

The shift

Movement became both my medicine and my teacher.

Yoga, acrobatics, breathwork, flexibility, dance, and somatic practices helped me experience healing as something I could feel, not just something I could think through.

Body

Presence became possible.

For the first time, I began to understand what it meant to be in my body instead of running from it. Movement helped me feel my emotions, listen inward, and experience strength and softness at the same time.

Breath

Softness stopped feeling like weakness.

Slow, grounding practices taught me that healing was not only about pushing through. It was also about pausing, breathing, receiving, resting, and letting myself be human.

Self

I started rebuilding trust with myself.

Piece by piece, I began letting go of the versions of myself I had created to survive. Not quickly. Not perfectly. Not always beautifully. But honestly.

Places that shaped me

Bali helped me feel seen. Thailand taught me how to see myself.

Bali, Indonesia

The moment I felt seen.

I went to Bali thinking I was there to deepen my acrobatics, to flip, fly, and live upside down. Instead, I found myself in slow, grounding yoga practices I used to roll my eyes at. What I thought would be “boring old yoga” became one of the most transformative parts of my life.

One moment I will never forget was an eye-gazing exercise. We were asked to look into a stranger’s eyes for fifteen minutes. No talking. No hiding. No looking away. That stranger looked at me with more compassion than I had ever shown myself. Something inside me softened. For the first time in my life, I felt seen.

Thailand

The moment I learned to listen.

In Thailand, I completed a 500-hour therapeutic yoga training. That experience deepened everything. Somatic awareness, breathwork, ritual, and slow intentional movement taught me that the body often knows the truth long before the mind is ready to say it out loud.

Thailand taught me how to stop abandoning myself. It taught me how to listen inward, how to honor what my body was holding, and how to rebuild a relationship with the parts of me I had learned to ignore.

The body held answers my mind could not touch.

Around the same time, I was studying psychology, earning my Master’s in Mental Health Counseling, and working across crisis, school-based, partial care, intensive outpatient, and behavioral health settings.

The more I learned clinically, the more I understood that healing could not live only in insight. Insight matters, but so does the body. So does the nervous system. So does emotion. So does the quiet, tender process of learning how to be with yourself differently.

Where I am now

Today, my life looks nothing like the girl who once doubted she belonged here.

I have a beautiful family, a grounded relationship with myself, and a life where movement, artistry, motherhood, creativity, and healing all intertwine.

I am a therapist, yes. But I am also a mover, performer, acrobat, dancer, aerialist, creator, and lifelong student of the body. I tell stories with movement now in ways I once could not tell with words.

Every chapter of my journey shaped me — my heart, my purpose, my work, and the way I sit with clients. It taught me how to help others come home to themselves with the same gentleness I had to learn from scratch.

Why this work matters

The people I work with often remind me of who I used to be.

The one who carried too much. The one who tried too hard. The one who kept everyone else together while quietly falling apart. The one who did not know how to love herself because no one had ever really taught her how.

I know what it is like to feel disconnected from yourself while still functioning. I know what it is like to perform okay. I know what it is like to crave softness but not know how to receive it.

That is why The Embodied Self exists. To offer the support, compassion, honesty, guidance, and space I once needed. A place where healing can be deep, human, creative, body-based, and real.

You are not broken. You are becoming.

If you are moving through a season where you feel lost, overwhelmed, disconnected, or far from who you truly are, I want you to know that there is nothing wrong with you. There is a way back to yourself. And you do not have to find it alone.
